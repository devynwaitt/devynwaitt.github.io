The Beautiful Future

The Beautiful Future is a science prediction narrative TV series about a young woman crippled by her mostly digital existence. She falls in love with an offline advocate who incites an offline awakening in her, amidst a society battling between techno-optimism and a return to the organic. Limited, it consists of eight scripted episodes, covering topics like privacy, automation, brain-computer interfaces, digital citizenship, machine intelligence, and entropy.

Tone Clip

A moving look book for the series, intended to establish the visual style and mood.

It is my intention to use tropes, and classic storytelling with pop sensibility, to communicate critical theory and critical design to a mainstream audience. “A spoonful of sugar,” is the way I have always thought about the commercial aspects of the series, and their role as a vehicle for grander ideas and criticisms about our relationship with technology.

Design Fiction Concepts
Dream House

Dream house is a virtual reality sandbox platform, a "virtual dollhouse" and social network that allows users to design a 3D virtual space.

It began as a critical design narrative device in The Beautiful Future, speaking to larger ideas about an increasingly simulated existence. In the series, the platform is ‘born from a tumblr and Pinterest merger after ownership of the VR headsets became ubiquitous,’ and the experience is highly addictive as users have access to virtual possessions they are unable to afford in real life. Our protagonist's mother slowly becomes addicted to the game, and throughout the story we see her real house fall into disrepair as she becomes a celebrity in the Dream House network.

Features include: social components (invite your friends to your house), allow for curators (tours and events in popular spaces), maker components (create your own design templates and objects for others to use). Minecraft meets Pinterest for the virtual age.

     


Mind Chat

Mind Chat is a form of communication through an augmented reality wearable device. Your interface appears projected onto the real world. The technology is based loosely on the speculative designs of the Magic Leap. The device responds to small motor movements, head and facial gestures, as well as hand and finger movement.

In the series, MC takes the form of critical theory, as Ava who communicates solely through MC has difficulty vocalizing and maintaining eye contact. While her MC communication is rapid-fire and eloquent, her verbal abilities are stunted.

 


Biometric Mood Rings

Something I find underrepresented in science fiction and speculative design, are some of the nuanced touches of femininity. So often we see the future painted as sleek, sterile, cold, and androgynous. By marrying the quantitative efficiency of monitoring biometric data, with the kitsch girliness of a mood ring, a transhuman object becomes relatable.

As a form of critical design, the ring works as a prescient data gathering device for advertisers. An example being the ring transmitting information about your dehydration to a refreshment company to advertise to you before you feel thirst.


 





Outsource

Developed initially to work with MC devices and then as an intracranial software, outsource allows you to place thoughts into a separate brain bank, to be accessed later. The software then crawls the internet and creates a customizable brief which you can either read, or have loaded directly back into your cognition.




No Nets

No-nets exist in The Beautiful Future as a form of rebellion against nearly ubiquitous internet. It is my interest with this series, to create critical design in the form of cultural attitudes, as well as through technology and objects. The no-net is representative of what I envision will be a growing anti-technology movement. It is a remote area set up to have no wi-fi, satellites or cameras, and is frequented by anarchist kids with a desire to disconnect.



Watching Me

I recorded my computer screen for one month, in an exploration of privacy. The objective was to see how I felt, and whether to not my behavior changed when I knew my digital actions were being recorded. Even though no one would watch without my consent, I observed the mere act of recording, without a viewer, created unease and self consciousness behind my actions. I was less likely to conduct ‘indulgent’ behavior, wasting time on social media or watching television shows; and visited sites and read articles in line with my professed interests. This project is part of an on-going study about the psychological effects of transparency. The video below is a compilation of the footage.

Proposals crafted at the request of the Tribeca Institute for experimental VR experiences.

Tropical Depression

Synopsis: We meet Jasmine at a point in her life where she is confused about who she is, and where her life is going. The story takes place almost entirely in her mind, as she walks down a country road to the gas station. By skipping along her neural pathways, a picture of her current predicament comes into focus. We discover a strained relationship with her new husband, romanticized versions of her past selves, and a desire to escape through visions of the future.

Artist statement: I have always been fascinated by the way my mind works, especially when writing. Something outside of my consciousness seems to bring related moments from my past to whatever I am currently trying to work through. Sometimes it is only after reading what I have written that I understand the ties. Thinking of this, which leads to that, sometimes bound by a color or feeling. Thinking about my parents breaking up, and remembering the red dress my mother wore sitting on the patio when she decided to leave; the same shade as a neon bar sign outside of my old apartment.

It is this swift movement through thoughts and memories that I aim to explore on Jasmine’s walk to the gas station. What is the mechanism behind your mind? How much of the external world propels it’s motion?

As opposed to presenting her struggle in a traditional narrative, it will be revealed through her thought patterns. The intimate nature of this approach calls to mind the William Blake quote, about infinity in a grain of sand. Or the Nicholson Baker novel, ‘The Mezzanine,’ the entirety of which takes place on the protagonist’s journey up an escalator.

By learning about Jasmine through her perception and linked memories, we go deeper than internal dialogue, nearly to the electricity traveling through her brain. Pairing this inner world with lush visuals of the external world, we feel truly inside of her human experience.

By filming the sequences in 360 video, we enable a viewer to experience Jasmine’s environments, with her thoughts swirling through the air. This takes us as close to inside of her mind as technology currently allows.

A subtext of the film, will be the seamless integration and effect of social networking on her thoughts. The impulse to, after seeing a beautiful flower, share it online as opposed to admire it is second nature. By being inside of her thoughts, we have a firsthand look at how ‘share’ and ‘like’ culture interrupts and infiltrates them. We see Jasmine’s already fragmented mind, further scattered by maintaining several different online identities.

This brings forth a point that she herself contemplates, which is the vulnerability created by our reliance on technology. Jasmine believes now, that her only escape is her mind, takes comfort in the infinity of it. But what happens when corporations that own a lifetime of your data, are able to predict your thoughts?

Her musings on the fate of humanity’s relationship with technology serve as form of escape from her own problems. And as a writer, her thoughts are a device. A way to present my own research, without having to conceal it in a narrative. It is a liberating and immediate way to express my own concerns about issues like net neutrality and the existential risk of artificial intelligence. By turning them into the thoughts of a character, I can present them as what they actually are - ideas.

The film climaxes with an imagined vision of herself as a goddess of liberty, in a world tattered and torn apart. The barriers of her mind breached, simulation exposed. We juxtapose this fearful fever pitch with a return to traditional narrative. No internal monologue, or wandering thoughts, a simple exchange with the man at the gas station. Showing how sometimes connection with a stranger, a human, is just what you need to get you out of your head.
